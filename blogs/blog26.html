Mind-controlled Prosthetics of the future

- by Joel Joseph

The world of prosthetics is developing quicker by the year. Doctors, engineers, and students are all striving to design more effective artificial limbs that can help amputees and sufferers of paralysis live not only more comfortable but also thrive. A prosthesis is an artificial device that replaces a missing part of the body, which may be lost as a result of trauma, illness, or a condition that occurs at birth. Prosthetics can be created by hand or with computer-aided design, a software interface that helps designers design and analyze creation. When God gifted us a pair of hands and legs, we never thought about the world without them, but to those people who don’t have limbs, it is difficult to do their daily life activities. For example, it’s difficult for amputees to open a bottle. So, for them, prosthetics is a gamechanger in their life.

Prosthetics were developed in the nineties, but they were heavy, huge, and very expensive. The problem with prosthetics is a longstanding one that researchers have been working on for many years. They are attempting to create a prosthetic that acts as if it is the original limb or body part. So, mind-controlled prosthetics came into existence, which is a very interesting concept. Prosthetics is transmitted through the stump of the arm and is thought-controlled via electrical muscle and neural signals recorded by the electrodes. The signal is sent to the implant, which passes through the skin and connects to the prosthesis.

This achievement is unique. Patients have been using mind-guided prostheses in their daily life for up to 7 years. For the past few years, they have also lived with new touch-sensing capabilities in their prosthetics. This is a new concept prosthetic leg called a neuromusculoskeletal prosthesis because it is connected to the user's nerves, muscles, and skeleton. It is directly related to nerves, muscles, and supervision.


The latest technology is touch sensation, which is made possible by stimulating nerves previously associated with biological arms before amputation. A force sensor on the thumb of the prosthesis measures contacts and pressure applied to an object while gripping. This information travels along the patient's nerves to the brain. Therefore, when the patient touches an object, he can feel the nature of the object and the degree of pressing, which is very important for simulating a biological hand.

How is mind-controlled prosthetics different from Conventional prosthetics? In traditional prosthetics, the implant attaches to the bones of the remaining extremities via osseointegration, which essentially extends the skeleton to a point where a prosthesis can be attached. Whereas in mind-controlled prosthetics, the key difference is that the electrical sensors used in myoelectric prostheses attach to the skin through an external socket, whereas the new mind-control osseointegration prostheses have all the wiring under the skin. Conventional prosthetic limbs can be uncomfortable and painful. A lot of people throw them away.

In the past, these prosthetics came with hefty price tags which were not affordable to many, but due to the technological advancement made by mankind, mainly using 3D printing, it will be sold to them at a price half of the traditional ones. These advances are a huge win for people who have lost a limb, who have long had limited opportunities to use prosthetics, many of whom have been uncomfortable and painful.
